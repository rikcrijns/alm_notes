Part A — Conceptual (4 pts)

A.1 — Define SCC in terms of $J$. What does $-J_S/J_C$ mean economically?
1 pt▼
✓ Model Answer

$\text{SCC}\equiv-J_S/J_C$. The numerator $-J_S>0$ is the welfare cost of one unit of carbon stock. Dividing by $J_C>0$ converts to consumption units. The ratio is the consumption the planner is willing to forgo to reduce $S$ by one unit — the consumption-equivalent price of carbon.

A.2 — "Physical risk is insurable, so only transition risk matters." Evaluate using DICE.
1 pt▼
✓ Model Answer

Flawed. Individual events may be insurable, but systemic chronic risk (sea-level rise, persistent drought) enters DICE as $D(T)$ in the production function — an economy-wide loss that no private insurer can offset. Also, insuring physical risk does not remove the externality.

A.3 — Write the optimal abatement FOC and interpret MAC = SCC × marginal emission reduction.
1 pt▼
✓ Model Answer

$$\theta_1\theta_2\mu^{\theta_2-1}Y=\text{SCC}\cdot|\partial E/\partial\mu|$$

LHS: cost of raising abatement by $d\mu$. RHS: value of the emission reduction in consumption units. At optimum, cost of abating = benefit of lower carbon, where benefit is valued at SCC.

A.4 — State Weitzman's rule. When does each carbon pricing mechanism dominate?
1 pt▼
✓ Model Answer

Prefer a carbon tax when $|\partial\,\text{MAC}/\partial q|>|\partial\,\text{MD}/\partial q|$ (MAC curve steeper). Prefer ETS when MD curve is steeper. Intuition: price certainty (tax) avoids extreme abatement costs when MAC is steep; quantity certainty (ETS) avoids irreversible damage when MD is steep.

Part B — Climate HJB (6 pts)

B.1 — Write the full Climate HJB including the Poisson jump term. Define every symbol.
2 pts▼
✓ Model Answer

$$\rho J=\max_C\!\left\{\frac{C^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}+J_K[Y-C-\delta_K K]+J_S[E-\delta_S S]+J_T\beta[\ln(S/S_\text{pre})-T]+\lambda\!\int_0^1[J(K(1-\varepsilon),S,T)-J(K,S,T)]f(\varepsilon)d\varepsilon\right\}$$

$\rho$=discount rate; $\gamma$=CRRA; $\lambda$=Poisson rate; $\varepsilon$=capital destruction fraction; $f(\varepsilon)$=severity density; $\delta_K,\delta_S$=depreciation; $\beta$=temperature adjustment speed.

B.2 — Conjecture $J=\frac{C^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}S^{-\gamma\varphi}$. Evaluate the jump integral and derive the closed-form SCC.
2 pts▼
✓ Model Answer

$J_C=C^{-\gamma}S^{-\gamma\varphi}$, $J_S=\frac{C^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}(-\gamma\varphi)S^{-\gamma\varphi-1}$. Under conjecture: $J(K(1-\varepsilon),\ldots)=(1-\varepsilon)^{1-\gamma}J$, so $\mathcal{L}_\text{jump}J=\lambda J\Phi(\gamma)$ where $\Phi(\gamma)=\int[(1-\varepsilon)^{1-\gamma}-1]f(\varepsilon)d\varepsilon<0$.

$$\text{SCC}=-J_S/J_C=\frac{\gamma\varphi}{1-\gamma}\cdot\frac{C}{S}$$

B.3 — Interpret $\text{SCC}=\frac{\gamma\varphi}{1-\gamma}\cdot\frac{C}{S}$. How does it depend on $\gamma$, $C/S$, $\lambda$? What if $\lambda\to\infty$?
2 pts▼
✓ Model Answer
  • ↑γ: $\frac{\gamma}{1-\gamma}$ rises → SCC rises. Higher risk aversion penalises catastrophic outcomes more.
  • ↑C/S: SCC rises. Richer economy relative to carbon burden → higher WTP for emission reduction.
  • ↑λ (via φ): More disasters → $\varphi$ rises → SCC rises. Greater physical risk raises the shadow cost of carbon.
  • λ→∞: $\varphi\to\infty$, SCC$\to\infty$. Certain catastrophe means any emission is infinitely costly.
